1 The Idea / October 2022

Before the agent, there was a loop

The whole field rests on a deceptively small idea: let a language model think out loud and act in the same breath, then feed it back what happened.

In October 2022 — a month before ChatGPT existed — a Princeton PhD student named Shunyu Yao and his co-authors posted a paper called ReAct: Synergizing Reasoning and Acting in Language Models. Until then, two abilities had been studied separately: a model could reason (chain-of-thought) or it could generate an action plan. ReAct interleaved them. At each step the model writes a thought (why), chooses an action (what), and reads back an observation (result) — then loops. Reasoning keeps the plan honest; actions let it reach outside its own context to a search engine, a calculator, an API.

That reason → act → observe cycle is still the beating heart of every agent shipped today. It's worth saying plainly, because the marketing has buried it: an "AI agent" is just a language model placed in a loop, given tools, and allowed to decide its own next step. Everything else — frameworks, protocols, multi-agent choreography — is scaffolding around that single primitive.

An agent is a model running in a loop with tools, choosing for itself when the work is done.

The one-sentence definition that survived four years of hype

ReAct was modest and academic. It would take a far bigger model — and a far louder moment — to convince the world that this loop could replace a junior employee.

2 The Spring / March–April 2023

The fantasy of the autonomous employee

On March 14, 2023, OpenAI released GPT-4. Sixteen days later, a game developer named Toran Bruce Richards pushed an experiment called AutoGPT to GitHub. The pitch was intoxicating: give the AI a goal in plain English, then walk away and come back to finished work. No prompts. No hand-holding. Not a chatbot — an employee.

It spread like nothing the developer world had seen. AutoGPT cleared 100,000 GitHub stars within weeks, becoming the top trending repository on the platform. Days later a venture investor, Yohei Nakajima, shipped BabyAGI — a cleaner take built from three cooperating roles: one agent to create tasks, one to prioritize them, one to execute. He had coded it, fittingly, by having GPT-4 help write it. For a few weeks in the spring of 2023, it genuinely felt like software had learned to hire itself.

Then people actually ran them.

The agents looped. They set themselves vague sub-goals, chased perfection, and never declared victory. They hallucinated steps, then built on the hallucinations. Small per-step error rates compounded across long task chains until the output was nonsense — and every iteration burned tokens at an alarming rate. AutoGPT's own team later found the elaborate vector-database memory most clones bolted on was overkill, and ripped it out for a plain local file. The promise of "walk away" collided with the reality that no one could trust what they'd come back to.

The quiet retirement

By mid-2024 the autonomous-agent boom had cooled. Nakajima archived BabyAGI in September 2024, relaunching it as an explicit research sandbox. AgentGPT's parent pivoted to web scraping. AutoGPT itself was rewritten into a visual workflow builder — useful, but, as one retrospective put it, a completely different product from the one that earned those hundred thousand stars.

The lesson that outlived the hype is the one every generation of technology relearns: the useful thing that emerges is never the thing that was promised. AutoGPT didn't deliver an autonomous worker; it delivered a workflow builder. BabyAGI didn't deliver autonomy; it delivered dozens of academic papers and an education for a generation of builders. What broke in the spring of 2023 wasn't the idea of the loop — it was the belief that you could leave the loop unsupervised.

3 The Reframe / 2024

From “autonomous agent” to “agentic workflow”

The recovery didn't come from a bigger model. It came from a change of vocabulary — and a change of ambition.

In March 2024, Andrew Ng gave the field a more honest frame. Instead of selling fully autonomous agents, he named four concrete agentic design patterns that reliably improved results: Reflection (the model critiques and revises its own output), Tool Use (calling out to code, search, APIs), Planning (decomposing a goal into steps), and Multi-agent collaboration (specialized roles checking each other). The shift was subtle but decisive: stop chasing a magical autonomous worker, and start composing a workflow in which the model takes several deliberate passes instead of one.

Ng's demonstration of choice was telling — code generation. Code has objective feedback: it runs or it doesn't, tests pass or fail. That made it the perfect proving ground for agentic patterns, and it's why coding became the first place agents genuinely worked. The vagueness that doomed "book me a vacation" was replaced by tasks with a verifier attached.

Reflection, tool use, planning, collaboration — not a robot employee, but a disciplined workflow with the model in the loop.

The 2024 reframe that pulled agents out of the trough

Underneath the vocabulary, an infrastructure layer was maturing too. Back in October 2022, an ML engineer named Harrison Chase had pushed a side project called LangChain — a library to kill the boilerplate of wiring models to data and tools. When ChatGPT launched weeks later, it became, briefly, the fastest-growing open-source project on GitHub, and the company raised $35M from Sequoia and Benchmark. But LangChain's original AgentExecutor — one rigid class that ran the autonomous loop — turned out to be exactly the wrong abstraction for production. Developers wanted control, not magic.

So the team built LangGraph: instead of a black-box loop, you model the agent as an explicit graph of steps and state, with checkpoints, memory, and human-in-the-loop pauses you can actually inspect. It was an admission, encoded in software, of the spring's hard lesson — that real agents need structure around the loop, not faith in it. CrewAI, Microsoft's AutoGen, and others staked out the same territory: give the loop guardrails, roles, and recovery, and it starts to hold up.

4 The Plumbing / Nov 2024 → 2025

The boring standards that made agents real

An agent is only as capable as the tools it can reach. Through 2024, connecting a model to Slack, a database, GitHub, or a CRM meant writing a bespoke connector for every model-and-tool pairing — an M×N integration nightmare. The fix arrived as infrastructure so unglamorous it's easy to miss how much it mattered.

MCP. In November 2024, Anthropic open-sourced the Model Context Protocol. Its origin story is refreshingly mundane: a developer, David Soria Parra, was tired of copying code back and forth between Claude Desktop and his IDE. MCP standardized that connection — one protocol any model could speak to any tool, widely described as “USB-C for AI.” The adoption curve was almost unheard-of for a standard. OpenAI added support across its products in March 2025; Google followed with Gemini in April. By early 2026, MCP was clearing tens of millions of SDK downloads a month with well over ten thousand community servers — and Anthropic had handed it to a vendor-neutral home, the Linux Foundation's new Agentic AI Foundation, in December 2025, with AWS, Google, Microsoft, OpenAI, Block, and Bloomberg all signed on.

A2A. MCP connects an agent to its tools. It says nothing about how two agents — built by different vendors, on different clouds — talk to each other. That's the gap Agent2Agent set out to fill. Google announced A2A in April 2025 with fifty-plus partners and donated it to the Linux Foundation by June. Its core idea is the Agent Card: a small published manifest where an agent advertises what it can do, so others can discover it, authenticate, and hand off a task. The two protocols are complementary, not rival — MCP is agent-to-tool, A2A is agent-to-agent — and a 2025 extension, AP2, even bolted a payments layer onto A2A so agents could transact.

An honest footnote on hype

Not every standard wins equally. MCP became the genuine de-facto layer — embedded everywhere, debated constantly. A2A's mindshare has been more uneven; some practitioners argued through 2025 that it had quietly faded next to MCP, even as the official count of supporting organizations passed 150. The reality is that agent-to-agent interop only becomes a blocking problem once you have real multi-agent systems in production — and most teams aren't there yet.

The deeper point: standardizing the plumbing did what every successful platform shift does. It let builders stop re-inventing connectors and start building on top. But it also created a new surface for trouble — by early 2026, security researchers had filed dozens of CVEs against MCP implementations, a reminder that “universal connector” also means “universal attack surface.”

5 The Architecture Wars / June 2025

One question, two giants, opposite answers

In the span of about a day in June 2025, two of the most respected teams in the field published essays with directly contradictory titles — and the disagreement turned out to be the most clarifying thing that happened to agent architecture all year.

Cognition (makers of Devin)
“Don't Build Multi-Agents”

Walden Yan's argument: parallel sub-agents make independent decisions, and independent decisions on the same problem produce conflicting, incoherent results. Keep it a single thread. Reliability comes from one agent with carefully managed context.

— published the same week —
Anthropic
“How we built our multi-agent research system”

A lead agent spawns sub-agents, each with its own clean context window and a self-contained task — they don't even know each other exist. For open-ended research, this beat a single agent by a wide margin, at roughly fifteen times the token cost.

Read closely, the two camps weren't really fighting — they were describing different jobs. The hinge is a distinction that now organizes the whole discipline: reading parallelizes; writing does not. Research is mostly reading — you can fan out a dozen sub-agents to gather sources in parallel and merge the findings. Coding is mostly writing — sub-agents editing the same codebase make conflicting changes that don't cleanly merge, so a single coherent thread wins. Anthropic's research system and Cognition's coding agent reached opposite architectures because they sit on opposite sides of that line.

Both teams converged on the same root cause of failure — and gave it a name: context engineering.

The real takeaway of the architecture wars

That phrase — context engineering, coined in Cognition's post — is the most important idea to come out of 2025. Even a brilliant model fails if you feed it the wrong context: too much, too little, stale, or poorly framed. A Berkeley team drove the point home with a taxonomy of fourteen distinct failure modes in multi-agent systems, most of them not reasoning failures at all but breakdowns in how context and coordination were managed. The conclusion the field reached, after two years of blaming the models: most agent failures are engineering failures.

6 What Actually Works / The coding proof

Where the loop finally held

If you want to know whether agents are real, don't read a survey — look at the one domain with an honest scoreboard. SWE-bench Verified is a benchmark of 500 human-reviewed real GitHub issues: not “write a sorting function,” but “navigate a forty-thousand-line codebase, find what broke, and produce a patch that passes the project's own tests.” It's about as close to a junior engineer's actual job as a benchmark gets.

What's quietly interesting is the shape of the winning products — and how thoroughly they abandoned the 2023 dream. They don't run unsupervised. They split along an autonomy spectrum: at one end, IDE-native, human-in-every-loop tools like Cursor, where you watch and approve as you go; at the other, async “assign-and-review” agents like Devin that work for minutes to hours and return a pull request for a human to vet. Claude Code and OpenAI's Codex sit between, terminal-first with explicit approval gates. None of them say “walk away.” Every one of them keeps a human at a checkpoint. The market discovered that supervised autonomy is the product — and most serious teams now run two or three of these tools side by side, matched to the task.

7 Where It Sits Today / 2026

From prompts to harnesses

Step back and the four-year story has a clean through-line: each time a paradigm couldn't deliver, the field relocated the rigor somewhere harder.

Prompt engineering (2022–24) was the first home for that rigor — Andrej Karpathy's quip that the hottest new programming language was English. Then single prompts stopped being enough, and rigor moved to context engineering (2025) — deciding what knowledge, history, and tool definitions to assemble into the window at each step. And as agents began running autonomously for long stretches, it moved again, to what 2026 calls harness engineering: building the whole environment around the model — the execution loop, the retries and recovery, the guardrails and linters, the verification, the memory, the human checkpoints. The slogan that captures the era is blunt: agents aren't hard; the harness is hard. If the model is a CPU and the context window is RAM, the harness is the operating system.

So how state-of-the-art is all this, honestly? The fairest answer is a balance sheet, not a verdict.

What genuinely works now

  • Verifiable, long-horizon work. Coding, research, SRE, advanced support — anywhere a result can be checked — now gets strong first drafts from agents.
  • The plumbing is solved. MCP for tools, A2A for agent hand-offs, both vendor-neutral. Connecting agents to the world is no longer bespoke.
  • Supervised autonomy. Human-in-the-loop checkpoints turned a flaky demo into something teams will actually run in production.
  • The model is rarely the bottleneck. Frontier models cleared the capability bar; the remaining problems are systems problems.

What's still genuinely hard

  • Integration, not intelligence. Reports put system integration — secure access to real production data — as the top blocker, ahead of model quality.
  • Security lags adoption. Only a small minority of agents reach production with full security sign-off; impersonation and credential attacks are climbing.
  • Reliability over length. Errors still compound on long runs; multi-agent setups add coordination failure modes of their own.
  • Uneven ROI. Analysts warn a large share of agentic projects risk cancellation before they pay off. Pilots are easy; durable value is not.

The enterprise mood in 2026 reflects exactly that split. Adoption is no longer the question — surveys find nearly every large enterprise planning to expand agent use, and analysts expecting a big share of business applications to embed task-specific agents within the year. But the conversation has moved decisively from can we build an agent to can we operate one reliably, securely, and at scale. The hard part isn't the demo anymore. It's the second Tuesday in production.

The autonomous employee never showed up. What arrived instead was a disciplined, supervised, well-engineered system — and that turned out to be the more valuable thing.

The quiet punchline of the agentic era

That's the real arc, and it's worth holding onto as the next topic's worth of hype arrives. The viral fantasy of 2023 was wrong about the destination but right about the loop. Everything since — the patterns, the frameworks, the protocols, the harnesses — has been the field doing the unglamorous work of making that loop trustworthy. Give it a goal and walk away was always the wrong instruction. Give it a goal, give it tools, and stay close is the one that shipped.
