AI Foundations · No. 03 — The Story of RAG
Deliverable #2  ·  The Story  ·  A researched long-read

The open-
book exam

A language model is a closed-book test: it can only recite what it memorized, and when it doesn't know, it makes things up. The fix was almost embarrassingly simple — let it look things up. This is the story of how that five-year-old idea, born with a name its own inventor apologized for, became the backbone of nearly every AI product shipped today.

Chapter I — The Closed Book

A brilliant student who can't look anything up

Picture the most well-read person you've ever met, sealed in a room with no books, no phone, no internet, and asked to answer any question on earth from memory alone. They'll be astonishing on what they happened to study and confidently, fluently wrong on everything else — and they'll never be able to tell you which is which. That is a large language model taking a closed-book exam. Its knowledge is frozen at the moment training stopped; it has never seen your company's documents; and when it reaches the edge of what it memorized, it doesn't stop — it confabulates, producing a plausible answer with no source behind it.

This is the failure mode underneath most of the trouble people have with LLMs: hallucination, stale knowledge, and no way to cite a source. None of it is a flaw in the model's intelligence. It's a flaw in the format of the test. A closed-book exam measures recall. Most real questions — which candidates did we place at Acme last week, what does our refund policy say, what changed in the contract — are open-book by nature. The answer isn't in the student's memory. It's in the filing cabinet.

The fix was never a smarter student. It was handing the same student the open book.

That is the entire idea of Retrieval-Augmented Generation, and it's why a system like Pulse — a placement bot answering from live candidate and role data the base model never saw — can't be built any other way. You don't retrain the model every time a candidate gets placed. You let it look the answer up at the moment it's asked. The hard part, as the next twenty years would prove, is everything hiding inside the words "look it up."

Chapter II — The 2020 Paper

Reading Wikipedia, and a name nobody loved

The instinct to let a model look things up was older than the transformer. In 2017, a system called DrQA was already reading Wikipedia to answer open-domain questions. But 2020 was the year three pieces snapped together. Dense Passage Retrieval showed that searching by meaning — encoding a question and a passage into vectors and comparing them — beat decades-old keyword search by a wide margin on question answering. Google's REALM baked retrieval directly into a model's pre-training. And then a team led by Patrick Lewis, working across Facebook AI Research, University College London, and NYU, unified the pieces into a single, general framework and gave it a name.

The paper was Retrieval-Augmented Generation for Knowledge-Intensive NLP Tasks, NeurIPS 2020. Its architecture is the one every RAG system still echoes: a parametric memory (a generator — they used BART) paired with a non-parametric memory (a dense vector index of Wikipedia). Ask a question, retrieve the most relevant passages, condition the generation on them. The result set a new state of the art on open-domain QA and fact verification — proof that a model with an open book produces more specific, more factual answers than one running on memory alone.

There's a small, telling footnote to the founding. Lewis has cheerfully apologized for the acronym. The team always meant to find something more elegant than “RAG,” but, as he later put it, when it came time to write the paper, nobody had a better idea. The name stuck — and went on to describe a whole family of techniques across hundreds of papers and dozens of products. A clunky three letters became one of the most load-bearing ideas in applied AI.

Chapter III — The Gold Rush

Chat with your docs

For two years RAG was a respected research result that most of the world had never heard of. Then, in late 2022, ChatGPT arrived and every company on earth asked the same question: can it answer from our data? The honest answer was no — not on its own, and not by pasting a knowledge base into a chat box, because there was a hard limit on how much text fit in a prompt. RAG was the bridge. It became the default architecture for “chat with your docs” almost overnight, and it has a shape worth committing to memory.

The naive RAG pipeline — query time

Build time: documents → chunk → embed → store in vector DB  ·  done once, ahead of any query

Split your documents into chunks, turn each into a vector, and store them. At query time, embed the question, pull the nearest chunks, paste them into the prompt, and let the model answer from them. Simple enough to build in a weekend — and a whole industry rushed in to sell the pieces. A vector-database gold rush followed: Pinecone, Weaviate, Chroma, Qdrant, Milvus, and Postgres's pgvector all competed to be the filing cabinet. Frameworks like LangChain and LlamaIndex turned the pipeline into a few lines of glue. By 2025, every major cloud — AWS, Google, Microsoft, IBM, NVIDIA, Oracle — was selling RAG as a managed service.

Why not just fine-tune?

Fine-tuning bakes knowledge into the weights — slow, expensive, and stale the moment the data changes. RAG keeps knowledge in an index you can update in seconds, and it can show you which document an answer came from. For anything that changes or needs a citation, retrieval wins.

It looked, for a moment, solved. You could stand up a demo that answered questions about your own documents in an afternoon, and it felt like magic. Then teams tried to ship those demos to real users — and discovered that the easy 80% had been hiding a brutal 20%.

Chapter IV — The Disillusionment

The demo that died in production

The dirty secret of RAG is that the generation half mostly works, and the retrieval half mostly doesn't. An LLM handed the right passages writes a good answer almost every time. The trouble is getting it the right passages — and a naive pipeline gets them wrong in a dozen quiet ways. The model is only ever as good as what you put in front of it, so retrieval is the bottleneck, and a confident answer built on the wrong chunk is worse than no answer at all.

The failures are unglamorous and everywhere. Chunking splits a single idea across two fragments, so neither retrieves cleanly. Pure vector search misses exact terms — a part number, a name — that keyword search would have nailed. Stuff too many chunks in and the model gets lost in the middle, a well-documented tendency to ignore information buried in the center of a long prompt. And every irrelevant passage you retrieve doesn't just waste space; it actively degrades the answer, pulling the model toward noise. For Pulse, that's the difference between surfacing the right candidate and confidently citing one who was never placed.

Building a RAG demo is a weekend. Making it production-grade is the whole job.

This was the disillusionment of 2023: a thousand impressive demos, a fraction of them survivable in front of real users. The gap between them had a single cause — naive retrieval — and closing it turned RAG from a recipe you followed into an engineering discipline you practiced.

Chapter V — Advanced RAG

Teaching the system to read better

If the retriever is the bottleneck, you widen the retriever. The years after the gold rush produced a toolkit — sometimes called “advanced RAG” — aimed squarely at getting the right chunks in front of the model and nothing else. None of it is exotic. All of it is the difference between a demo and a system.

The advanced-RAG toolkit — fixing the retriever
RERANK
Re-order by relevance
Pull many chunks fast, then a cross-encoder re-scores the top few precisely.
HYBRID
Keyword + vector
Combine BM25 and dense search so exact terms and meaning both land.
REWRITE
Fix the query first
Expand or rephrase the question (HyDE) before searching, not after.
CHUNK
Smarter splitting
Semantic, sentence-window, parent-document — keep whole ideas intact.

A reranker lets you cast a wide net cheaply and then spend a more precise model on just the finalists, reordering them by true relevance. Hybrid search runs keyword and vector retrieval together, so a query that hinges on an exact string and one that hinges on meaning both succeed. Query rewriting — including tricks like generating a hypothetical answer and searching with that — fixes the question before it ever hits the index. And smarter chunking stops cutting ideas in half. Underneath all of it sits the discipline the bootcamp's sibling topic is built on: evaluation. Frameworks like RAGAS made retrieval measurable — faithfulness, context precision and recall — so a team could finally answer the question that haunted every naive pipeline: did that change actually help, or did it just feel like it?

The shift in one line

Naive RAG asks “what's nearest?” Advanced RAG asks “what's actually relevant, and how would I know?” The leap is from a similarity lookup to a measured retrieval system.

Chapter VI — “Is RAG Dead?”

The obituary that keeps getting written

There is a headline that returns on a schedule, every time context windows grow: is RAG dead? It ran when Claude reached 100K tokens, again when Gemini claimed two million, and again in 2026 when GPT-5 shipped million-token windows in production. The logic is seductive — if you can pour an entire encyclopedia into a single prompt, why bother chunking, embedding, and retrieving at all? Just give the model everything.

The recurring debate
The long-context claim
“Just put everything in the prompt.”
Windows are huge now. No pipeline, no vector DB, no retrieval bugs — the model reads it all and answers.
The RAG reality
“Everything is slow, costly, and still wrong.”
You pay per token on every call, latency climbs, the index updates but the prompt doesn't, and long context still degrades and still hallucinates.
Where it lands

Not rivals — complementary. Retrieval narrows; long context reasons over what's left.

The rebuttal has held every time, for reasons that compound. Cost and latency: a million-token prompt is billed and processed on every single query, where an index is searched in milliseconds. Freshness: the index updates the instant your data changes; the context you hand-assemble does not. Provenance: RAG can tell you which document an answer came from — a long prompt can't tell you why it said what it said, which is disqualifying in regulated work. Scale: no window, however vast, holds a corporation's trillion documents. And the quiet one: even with everything in context, models still lose the middle and still confabulate. Research weighing retrieval against long context keeps reaching the same verdict — they're complementary. The right frame was never “which wins.” It was “retrieval narrows the haystack; the big window reasons over what survives.”

Chapter VII — Where It Sits Today

From a pipeline to a loop

The deepest change in RAG by 2026 isn't a better embedding model. It's that retrieval stopped being a fixed pipeline that runs once per question and became a step in a reasoning loop the model itself controls. In agentic RAG, the LLM decides when to retrieve, what to search for, and whether the chunks it got back are good enough — or whether it should rewrite the query and try again. Patterns like Self-RAG and corrective RAG let the system critique its own retrieval mid-flight. The retriever went from a conveyor belt to a tool an agent picks up when it judges that it needs to.

Two other frontiers matter. GraphRAG, pioneered at Microsoft, builds a knowledge graph over your corpus so the system can answer the questions plain vector search chokes on — multi-hop chains and “summarize everything about X” questions that require connecting many documents, not finding one. And the whole activity is being re-christened context engineering: retrieval as one move among many an agent makes to assemble exactly the right context — writing, compressing, selecting, isolating — for the task in front of it.

What RAG solved

  • Grounding — answers anchored in real sources, not just the model's memory.
  • Freshness — update an index in seconds; no retraining to learn new facts.
  • Provenance — show the document an answer came from. Auditable by design.
  • Private knowledge — a general model answering from your proprietary data.

What's still hard

  • Retrieval quality — still the ceiling; a system is only as good as what it finds.
  • Multi-hop & global — connecting many documents is what GraphRAG is still chasing.
  • Knowing when to retrieve — and when to stop, in an agentic loop.
  • Security — poisoned or conflicting sources can quietly steer the answer.

Stand back and the shape of it is clear. The 2020 idea — a model with a frozen memory, handed an open book at the moment of the question — was never replaced by anything. Long context didn't kill it; agents didn't kill it. It absorbed each new capability and became something closer to infrastructure: the default way you give an AI system access to knowledge it wasn't born with. Roughly a majority of production AI applications now lean on it in some form.

The student never got a bigger memory. They got better at using the library.

Which is exactly why the RAG bootcamp earns its place as the template for everything else in this series. Embeddings, chunking, retrieval, reranking, and the discipline of measuring whether any of it worked aren't a single topic among many — they're the machinery underneath most things people actually build with AI. A system like Pulse isn't an application of a language model so much as a careful answer to one deceptively small instruction the whole field has spent five years unpacking: look it up.
